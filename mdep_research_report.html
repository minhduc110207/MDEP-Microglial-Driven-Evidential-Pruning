Microglial-Driven Evidential Pruning (MDEP):
Resolving Training Stagnation in Dirichlet Landscapes

Technical Report on Optimization and Stability
Abstract—This technical report documents the mathematical corrections and optimization strategies implemented to resolve the "Zero-Evidence Flat Landscape" anomaly encountered during the training of the Microglial-Driven Evidential Pruning (MDEP) framework. By severing the computational graph at the focal weight, modifying the Astrocyte signal derivation via forward hooks, and employing a multi-stage optimization warm-up sequence, the network successfully circumvents the gradient vanishing problem inherent to Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) on highly imbalanced medical datasets (ISIC 2024). Furthermore, an expanded clinical evaluation suite was integrated to rigorously validate the model's predictive uncertainty and hardware-level sparsity compliance.

I. Introduction

The MDEP framework integrates Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) with Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) to achieve uncertainty-aware, hardware-efficient neural networks. However, initial empirical evaluations on the highly imbalanced ISIC 2024 dataset revealed severe training stagnation. The loss function remained frozen at approximately 0.25, and the gradient norm collapsed to near-zero values. This report details the theoretical misalignments that caused this collapse and the algorithmic interventions that restored network convergence.

II. Mathematical Corrections

A. Focal Weight Gradient Detachment

In Evidential Focal Loss, the focal weight is defined as \((1-\hat{p}_c)^\gamma\), where the predicted probability is \(\hat{p}_c = \frac{\alpha_c}{S}\). When the network computes the backward pass, standard autograd engines allow gradients to flow through this focal weight term. Mathematically, this forces the optimizer to manipulate the Dirichlet distribution structure to artificially lower the loss, immediately collapsing the evidence \(e\) to zero.

To rectify this, the computational graph is explicitly severed at the focal weight, treating it strictly as a batch-wise scalar modifier:

focal_weight = (1.0 - p_target.detach()) ** gamma

B. Astrocyte Signal Interception

The Astrocyte agent dictates the growth phase of DST by evaluating the Epistemic Uncertainty relative to neuron activations, denoted as \(\nabla_{a^{(l)}} u_e\). The legacy implementation incorrectly queried the gradients of the weights directly via u_e.backward(). This fundamentally violated the MDEP theorem.

The system now employs PyTorch Forward Hooks to cache activation tensors \(a^{(l)}\) during the forward pass, enabling the exact calculation of activation-based gradients for precise synaptic regrowth.

C. Evidence Layer Initialization

Standard Xavier or Kaiming initializations yield excessively large pre-activation values. Passed through a Softplus function, this generates massive initial evidence (\(e \approx 100\)). The Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence penalty severely punishes this perceived "overconfidence," triggering explosive gradients that destroy the network.

To mitigate this, the weights of the terminal linear layer are initialized from a highly constrained normal distribution:

nn.init.normal_(weight, mean=0, std=0.001)

D. Optimizer Hijacking and Manual Momentum

A critical architectural flaw identified was "Optimizer Hijacking," where the AdamW optimizer automatically seized control of the latent scores matrix. This subjected the scores to weight decay, artificially compressing the topological memory and destroying the Multi-Agent System's deliberate updates. To resolve this, the latent scores were explicitly filtered out of the optimizer's parameter list. To satisfy the biased stochastic approximation theorem necessary for structural convergence, an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) momentum tracking buffer (\(\beta = 0.9\)) was manually implemented inside the agent update loop, ensuring smooth gradient trajectories without destructive weight decay.

E. Localized 2:4 Smoothed STE

The original Smoothed Straight-Through Estimator (STE) utilized a global sigmoid threshold. This failed to accurately reflect the highly localized nature of the NVIDIA 2:4 sparsity constraint, where only blocks of 4 contiguous weights compete. Consequently, the global STE transmitted gradients to incorrect elements. A "Localized STE" was implemented by reshaping the scores into blocks of 4, dynamically calculating the exact 2:4 survival boundary \(\tau = \frac{s_2 + s_3}{2}\) per block, and shifting the sigmoid center to \(\tau\). This ensures gradients are exclusively directed to weights actively competing at the sparsity boundary.

III. Optimization Stability

Escaping the flat landscape near \(e=0\) requires careful management of gradient magnitudes and momentum.

A. Focal Loss Scaling and Linear Decay

Because the initial uniform prediction yields \(p=0.5\), the focal term \((1-0.5)^2 = 0.25\) artificially shrinks gradient magnitudes by a factor of 4. A 4.0x multiplier is introduced to the final loss scalar to counteract this shrinkage. However, maintaining this 4.0x multiplier alongside the momentum gathered by the AdamW optimizer causes severe overshooting, pushing the network past the loss minimum and destabilizing the training. To resolve this, a linear decay schedule was implemented, smoothly transitioning the loss multiplier from 4.0 down to 1.0 during the 5-epoch warm-up period. This dynamic "thrust reduction" allows the inherent learning rate to take over guidance precisely when the network begins extracting valid features.

B. KL Divergence Annealing

Applying the full theoretical KL penalty immediately forces the untrained network to predict uniform distributions to avoid regularization. An annealing coefficient, \(\min(1.0, \frac{\text{epoch}}{10})\), was implemented to gradually introduce the penalty, allowing the network to extract preliminary feature representations.

C. Continuous Batch-Level Learning Rate Warm-up and Clipping

To allow the AdamW optimizer to safely build variance estimates during the initial turbulent gradient updates (where GradNorm exceeds 6.0), a 5-epoch linear warm-up from \(10^{-6}\) to \(10^{-3}\) was applied. Crucially, the learning rate interpolation is executed at the batch-level (step-level) rather than the epoch-level. This continuous, intra-epoch ramp-up ensures a smooth acceleration trajectory, preventing the disruptive gradient shocks that occur with discontinuous epoch-level steps. Simultaneously, gradients are strictly clamped at a maximum norm of 2.0 to prevent gradient explosions during the annealing phase.

D. Data Pipeline and Hardware Bottleneck Optimization

To eliminate severe GPU starvation anomalies caused by sequential CPU data loading, the data pipeline was overhauled to use asynchronous loading (multiple workers with pin_memory=True and memory prefetching). This ensures the GPU remains fully saturated. Furthermore, to overcome memory bandwidth limits, Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) was integrated. By casting the dense ResNet layers to high-speed 16-bit Float formats while explicitly reserving 32-bit Float precision for the sensitive Evidential Loss operations (log, digamma, and exponential functions), training time is drastically reduced without inducing numerical underflow or NaNs in the Dirichlet distribution.

E. Gradient Integrity and Mixed Precision Stabilization

During early training phases, an anomalous behavior was observed where the reported Gradient Norm consistently collapsed to NaN despite the loss behaving normally. A deep structural analysis revealed a triple-layered anomaly. First, the PyTorch Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) scaler naturally overflows in early batches; however, the script accumulated these initial NaN values into a running average, permanently poisoning the training logs. Second, the clip_grad_norm_ function was blindly applied to the entire model parameter space, forcing it to evaluate the latent scores matrix—which, being detached from the optimizer to prevent Optimizer Hijacking, retained an unscaled, astronomically large gradient (due to the AMP loss scale). This dominated the global gradient clipping calculation, effectively squashing valid gradients to zero. Finally, implicit precision downcasting by the autocast context occasionally forced critical Evidential Loss mathematical functions (e.g., torch.digamma) back into FP16, inducing numerical underflow. These issues were resolved by strictly isolating the Evidential Loss in an autocast(enabled=False) block, mapping gradient clipping exclusively to the optimizer's actively tracked parameter groups, and shielding the running average accumulator from invalid entries.

F. Divergence Illusion and Extreme Imbalance Mitigation

During the early epochs on the highly imbalanced ISIC 2024 dataset, a "Divergence Illusion" was observed where the training loss and gradient norms appeared to escalate continuously (\(0.17 \rightarrow 0.55\) for loss, \(2.3 \rightarrow 10.2\) for gradients). This was identified as a mathematical artifact of double-compensation: both Evidential Focal Loss and extreme class weights (e.g., 200x penalty for minority classes) were simultaneously penalizing the network. As the model rapidly learned to confidently predict the majority class, its Dirichlet strength (\(S\)) approached infinity. Encountering a rare minority sample caused the logarithmic penalty \(\log(S)\) to explode, driving the running average loss upwards despite the model learning correctly. Three countermeasures were deployed: (1) Evidence Clamping: The Softplus activation output was explicitly clamped to a maximum of 20.0, establishing a ceiling on overconfidence and preventing infinite logarithmic penalties. (2) Dampened Class Weights: Class weights were softened using a square root function, reducing the minority penalty multiplier from 200x to ~14x to avoid conflicting with the native Focal Loss dynamics. (3) Exponential Moving Average (EMA) Logging: The logging mechanism was refactored from a cumulative running average to an EMA format, ensuring that the printed metrics smoothly and accurately reflect the network's current state without historical drag.

G. Resolving Topological Freezing and Memory Drift

During deep structural validation, a critical logic flaw named Dense Gradient Masking was identified. Because PyTorch evaluates the forward path using effective_weight = weight * mask, the chain rule automatically multiplies the backpropagated gradient of the loss by the binary mask. This meant that all pruned connections (\(\text{mask} = 0\)) had their weight gradients forced to exactly zero. Consequently, the Astrocyte agent’s regrowth drive, calculated as \(G_{ij} = |\nabla u_e| \times |\nabla L|\), was completely blinded, locking the network into a rigid, non-exploratory topology. To resolve this, intermediate tensor gradient retention (retain_grad()) was implemented directly on the effective_weight tensor. By capturing and caching gradients from effective_weight.grad rather than the leaf parameters, we restored the dense gradient map, letting the Astrocyte accurately evaluate the prospective loss reduction of pruned weights. Additionally, because the multi-agent reward \(\Delta S = C_{ij} + G_{ij}\) is strictly non-negative and weight decay was bypassed on scores to preserve structural memory, the scores would naturally drift to positive infinity. This was mitigated by applying a Zero-Centering Stabilization step (scores.sub_(scores.mean())) after each update, preserving 100% of the topological ranking information while keeping the score mean perfectly anchored at zero.

IV. Advanced Evaluation Framework

To validate the performance of MDEP on the ISIC 2024 dataset, a comprehensive suite of clinical and hardware metrics was established.

A. Clinical Metrics for Extreme Imbalance

Given the extreme rarity of positive melanoma cases, traditional ROC-AUC is highly deceptive. The evaluation now prioritizes the Precision-Recall Area Under Curve (PR-AUC) and the Macro F1-Score. Furthermore, Sensitivity and Specificity are explicitly decoupled to monitor False Negative rates, a critical factor in oncological diagnostics.

B. Evidential Uncertainty Metrics

The framework computes the Brier Score, a strictly proper scoring rule that penalizes the absolute distance between predictions and true labels. Additionally, the Area Under the Risk-Coverage (AURC) curve is generated to quantify the model's ability to maintain high accuracy when rejecting low-confidence predictions.

C. Hardware Acceleration Metrics

The network dynamically prints a Sparsity Report during inference, verifying strict compliance with NVIDIA's Ampere 2:4 structured sparsity pattern. An analytical heuristic estimates the percentage of Multiply-Accumulate (MACs) operations saved, projecting theoretical speedups on Tensor Cores.

D. Theoretical Convergence Verification and Diagnostics

To explicitly prove the mathematical guarantees of the MDEP framework, robust real-time diagnostic modules were integrated. First, the Mask Flop Rate tracks the percentage of structural connections flipped (Microglia pruning vs. Astrocyte regrowth) per update, empirically demonstrating structural convergence as the flop rate decays to zero.

Second, acknowledging that 2:4 sparsity mathematically locks physical layer collapse at 50%, the framework redefines collapse as Representational Collapse—the loss of the multi-agent system's ability to rank connections, resulting in blind random pruning. A specialized diagnostic subroutine continuously monitors the latent score matrix \(S\) for three fatal conditions: Zero Variance (score standard deviation \(\le 10^{-4}\)), Negative Drift (score mean \(\le -10.0\), causing sigmoid saturation), and Dead Gradient (gradient norm \(\le 10^{-6}\), isolating the layer from Astrocyte signals). This ensures the topology remains active and theoretically sound throughout training.

V. Conclusion

Through the strategic detachment of focal weights, precise Astrocyte signal extraction, and rigorous optimization stabilization, the MDEP framework has successfully overcome the theoretical barriers of Evidential Deep Learning. The network now exhibits robust gradient flow, strict adherence to 2:4 sparsity, and provides highly calibrated uncertainty metrics essential for clinical deployment.
